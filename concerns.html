Concerns

Things that could go wrong — and how we handle each one.

Thinking ahead is not being negative

Knowing about a problem before it happens means we are not surprised by it. Every concern below has a plan. Nothing here should stop us from starting.

High priority
Gas shortage in Bangalore
This is real and ongoing. Our whole business depends on cooking, which depends on gas.

How we handle it: Always keep one backup cylinder at home. Never start cooking if there is uncertainty about gas that morning. In the worst case — take a day off rather than risk half-cooked food. Consider noting this on our Instagram so regular customers understand.
Overcommitting orders
The temptation to say yes to every order is real. But if aunty cooks 15 portions when she is only comfortable with 8, the quality will drop and she will burn out.

How we handle it: Aunty sets a daily maximum. We post "sold out for today" when that number is reached. This also creates a sense of demand and urgency which is actually good for the brand.
Medium priority
Bangalore heat in May
May is hot. Food — especially fish — can spoil faster than expected during delivery.

How we handle it: Deliver only within 2 hours of packing. Fish orders only for customers within 15–20 minutes delivery distance. Pack tightly and securely. Deliver before 1:30 PM before the afternoon heat peaks.
Delivery cost eating into profits
Rapido and Uber fees add up, especially for multiple small deliveries in different directions.

How we handle it: Charge a flat ₹30–40 delivery fee per order. As orders grow, see if customers in the same building or area can be batched into one delivery. Keep delivery zone tight — Malleshpalya and Kaggadasapura only.
Ingredient prices fluctuating
Fish prices in particular can change week to week. What costs ₹150 today might cost ₹220 next week.

How we handle it: Review the menu prices every 2–3 weeks. Build a small buffer into the fish pricing. If costs spike significantly, update the price list and announce it on Instagram with a short explanation. Customers respect honesty.
Worth knowing about
Slow order days at the start
The first two weeks will likely be slow. That is completely normal. Word of mouth takes time.

How we handle it: Do not panic. Keep posting consistently. The goal in Week 1 is not profit — it is learning the process and getting the first few happy customers who will then tell others.
A customer complains about the food
It will happen at some point. Even the best cooks get complaints.

How we handle it: Respond calmly and politely. Ask what was wrong. If the complaint is valid — offer a replacement next time. Do not argue. One handled complaint well can turn a critic into a loyal customer.
Your own energy and mental health
You started this partly to have something to focus on. But it should not become another source of stress.

How we handle it: Marketing is 30–45 minutes a day maximum. If a day is hard — skip posting. Put up a "closed today" story. The business will still be there tomorrow. Your wellbeing comes before every order.

The bottom line

None of these concerns are dealbreakers. They are just things to keep an eye on. The fact that we have thought about them means we are already ahead of most small businesses that start without any planning at all.
